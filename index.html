ICS Security in the Age of Industry 4.0

Think about water treatment plants, power generation plants, manufacturing units and similar other industrial plants. 'ICS' stands for Industrial Control System, a cyber-physical system that controls the operations of these plants.

Imagine waking up on a cold winter morning to find your heater not working. You walk into your kitchen to grab a glass of water, but find the taps running dry. You go to the supermarket to grab a block of cheese for your sandwich, but find all the dairy shelves empty. Sounds dystopian, right? Yet, thanks to recent cyberattacks on critical infrastructure, this isnâ€™t science fiction anymore. IT'S ALREADY HAPPENING!
Remember the Sandstorm attack on Ukrainian power grids in 2022, Texas water treatment plant attack in 2024, or the Scheriber Foods cyberattack in 2024 ? These attacks disrupt ICS operations, leading to severe civilian inconvenience, potential safety issues, cascading effects on downstream processes, operational downtime ranging from hours to weeks, and financial losses for plant owners that can reach upto millions of dollars. What's more concerning is that all these attacks have been executed remotely, sometimes from as far as across the globe!

That's true! While a section of the ICS security community keeps working on mitigating existing threats, in this project, we focus on identifying and mitigating emerging security threats in Industry 4.0. To be specific, we focus on Denial of Service attacks -- one of the most popular and notorious attacks known in the ICS domain.

Industry 4.0 refers to the Fourth Industrial Revolution, which integrates advanced technologies like IoT, AI, Internet connectivity, and high degrees of automation into manufacturing and industrial processes. It enables smart, interconnected systems that can be monitored and controlled remotely, analyzed, and optimized in real time, leading to greater efficiency, flexibility, and resilience. This also enables scaling up industrial operations. Have you seen the new water treatment plant of Mexico City?


In short, these modern plants have a higher degree of Internet connectivity in their critical infrastructure that makes them significantly more vulnerable to remotely executed attacks as compared to their traditional counterparts.
To understand this clearly, let's take a deeper look into the ICS network architecture. An ICS comprises two networks - an Information Technology (IT) network, and an Operational Technology (OT) network. The IT network is used for business operations, while it's the OT network that's responsible for connecting the critical cyber-physical devices that executes the industrial process (e.g., water treatment).
Traditionally, only the IT network used to be connected to the Internet, while the OT components used to be connected over a private network. The IT and OT network were also connected over a private network. This made the OT network relatively secure to remote adversaries, as they had to infiltrate the OT network through the IT network, and fortifying the IT network was enough to prevent OT invasions. But to facilitate scalability of operations and remote monitoring and control, some critical OT components are now being migrated to the cloud. This includes the Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition System (SCADA), which is responsible for coordinating among and synchronizing the operations of various remotely located OT components. This means the communications between the SCADA and other OT components are now transmitted over the Internet, making them exposed to remote adversaries.

We set out to explore a simple but important question: Is Internet-borne OT traffic vulnerable to remotely executed Denial of Service (DoS) attacks? Unlike IT traffic, OT traffic is extremely time- and safety-critical. Delayed or dropped packets in an ICS OT system isn't just inconvenient, it can be dangerous. Thatâ€™s why plant operators take cybersecurity seriously. Following NIST guidelines, they implement encryption, VPNs, volumetric DoS detection, and even network delay tolerance techniques to handle benign Internet latency and packet loss. But as we dug deeper, a question kept nagging us: Is all this really enough? In an attempt to find an answer, we did the following.

  1. First, we needed a realistic testbed to investigate the security of Internet-borne OT traffic. In absence of such a state-of-the-art testbed, we built our own in-house testbed. It mimics a 6-stage water treatment plant, similar to the physical one hosted at SUTD Singapore, except that the SCADA is now hosted on the cloud, and it incorporates state-of-the-art security measures.

  2. Second, we studied the characteristics of OT traffic and investigated its susceptibility to DoS attacks. We found that while the state-of-the-art ICS security measures thwart known DoS attacks, OT communications are vulnerable to a novel targeted DoS attack. We built a prototype of this attack and named it ICS-Sniper. This is concerning as ICS-Sniper can be executed from outside of the ICS perimeter, by remote adversaries, and can break the synchorization among its mechanical components, thereby disrupting ICS operations. Much like a sniper attack, it is highly precise and undetectable. Stay tuned here to know more about ICS-Sniper.

As we continue exploring the full potential of ICS-Sniper by testing it across diverse scenarios and developing practical countermeasures, we are also committed to raising awareness about this emerging threat within the ICS security community. We presented ICS-Sniper at the 2nd International Workshop on Re-design Industrial Control Systems with Security (RICSS) in 2024, hosted by the ACM CCS conference. We were also recently awarded a research funding by the National Cybersecurity Consortium (NCC) of Canada. We will be talking about the current state of our work at the NCC Conference in Banff on June 26 and 27, 2025.

To deepen our understanding and ground our research in real-world contexts, we are actively seeking collaboration with industries operating safety-critical infrastructure. Your insights into network architecture, operational constraints, and security priorities can help shape stronger, more resilient defenses for the future.
